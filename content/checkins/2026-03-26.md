## The Problem

Why must they meet? Once both pointers are inside the cycle, fast closes the gap by exactly **1 node per step**. So they are guaranteed to collide — no "skipping over" can happen.

```python
slow = fast = head
while fast and fast.next:
    slow = slow.next
    fast = fast.next.next
    if slow == fast:
        break  # collision inside the cycle
```

## Phase 2: Finding the Cycle Entry

After the collision, reset **one** pointer to head. Move both at speed 1. They meet at the cycle entry.

```python
def detectCycle(head):
    slow = fast = head
    while fast and fast.next:
        slow = slow.next
        fast = fast.next.next
        if slow == fast:
            slow = head            # reset slow to head
            while slow != fast:
                slow = slow.next   # both move at speed 1
                fast = fast.next
            return slow            # cycle entry
    return None
```

This feels like magic. Let's prove it.

When slow and fast collide, slow has walked exactly **k** steps:

- **(k − m)** steps from head to cycle entry
- **m** steps from cycle entry to the meeting point

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **k** | Total steps slow has taken when they meet |
| **m** | Distance from cycle entry to meeting point (along the cycle) |
| **r** | Length of the cycle |

### Step 2: Fast walked 2k steps — extra k steps are full laps

Fast has walked **2k** steps (twice slow's distance). They're at the same node, so the extra **k** steps are **entirely within the cycle** — just full laps.

This means **k is an integer multiple of the cycle length**:

> k = n × r &nbsp;&nbsp; (for some positive integer n)

### Step 3: The key insight

We need to show that the distance from **head to cycle entry** equals the distance from **meeting point to cycle entry** (going forward around the cycle).

**From head to cycle entry:** `k − m` steps.

**From meeting point to cycle entry** (forward around the cycle): the meeting point is `m` steps past the entry. Going forward `r − m` steps reaches the entry. But since `k = n×r`:

> n×r − m = k − m

**They are the same distance.** This is the whole trick.

### Step 4: Therefore...

Place one pointer at **head** and one at the **meeting point**. Advance both by 1 step at a time. After exactly **k − m** steps, they converge at the cycle entry.

**Phase 1** — fast/slow chase:

| Step | slow | fast |
|------|------|------|
| 0 | 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 3 | 6 |
| 4 | **4** | **4** |

They meet at node **4**. Now `k = 4`, meeting point is `m = 1` step past cycle entry.

**Phase 2** — reset slow to head, both advance at speed 1:

| Step | slow (from head) | fast (from node 4) |
|------|------------------|---------------------|
| 0 | 0 | 4 |
| 1 | 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 2 | 6 |
| 3 | **3** | **3** |

They meet at node **3** — the cycle entry. `k − m = 4 − 1 = 3` steps.

## Frequently Confused Points

**Q: What if slow goes around the cycle multiple times before meeting fast?**

Slow never completes a full lap before meeting fast. Once slow enters the cycle, fast is already inside and closing the gap by 1 node per step. The maximum gap is `r − 1`, so they meet within `r − 1` steps — before slow finishes one full revolution.

**Q: What if the head itself is part of the cycle?**

Then `k − m = 0`. The distance from head to cycle entry is zero. Both pointers start at (or immediately converge to) the cycle entry. The math still holds.

**Q: Why not just use a hash set?**

You can — O(n) time and O(n) space. Floyd's achieves O(n) time and **O(1) space**.

---

## The Complete Solution

```python
def detectCycle(head):
    fast, slow = head, head
    while fast and fast.next:
        fast = fast.next.next
        slow = slow.next
        if fast == slow:
            break

    # No cycle
    if not fast or not fast.next:
        return None

    # Reset slow to head, advance both at speed 1
    slow = head
    while slow != fast:
        fast = fast.next
        slow = slow.next
    return slow  # cycle entry
```

**Time:** O(n) — each pointer traverses at most the full list twice.
**Space:** O(1) — only two pointer variables.

---

## Takeaway

The beauty of Floyd's cycle detection isn't the code — it's the math. The key equation **k = n×r** (slow's total steps equal a multiple of the cycle length) creates a symmetry: the distance from head to cycle entry equals the distance from the meeting point to cycle entry. Once you see that, the "reset and walk together" phase feels inevitable, not magical.
